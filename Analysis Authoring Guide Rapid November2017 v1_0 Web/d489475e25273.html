Class Elements
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
Class Elements represents a defined information or data concept.
After p in entry
Class Elements are represented as a rectangular box, with the name of the Class inside it.
The name of a class should be meaningful in the context of the domain. The use of camel case for the class name is optional.
A brief description clearly explaining the purpose and nature of the Class should also be added to the element within its metadata.
A class may be split into three sections:
• 
Top section - Class name.
• 
Middle section - Attributes.
• 
Third section - Operations.
After p in entry
Class — Attributes:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
Attributes define the properties of individual data entities within a class.
Class Attributes can have their own properties applied to them (e.g. Datatypes).
Attribute Multiplicities show how many instances of each Attribute must / may exist for that Class.
After p in entry
A Class’s Attributes are captured as metadata content within the Element.
They can be displayed as a list in the second section of the Class Element.
Attribute names should be meaningful and use ‘camel case’ notation.
Class Attributes can be classified as being Public (+), Private (-), Protected (#) or Package — shown in front of the Attribute’s name.
The attribute information can include:
• 
A short description of the attribute (recommended).
• 
Multiplicities notation: [n..n] . A multiplicity of [1..1] is the default and not displayed
After p in entry
Class — Operations:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
Class Operations represent the behaviour or services that a Class supports.
Operations can be applied to a Class or its Attributes.
After p in entry
Operations are captured as metadata content within the Class Element
They can be displayed as a list in the third section of a Class Element.
After p in entry
Abstract Class
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
An Abstract Class represent abstract characteristics (attributes and / or operations) which are inherited or extended by its ‘child’ classes when they are instantiated (Abstract Classes are not instantiated).
After p in entry
An Abstract Class is represented as a Class Element, with its name shown in italics on Class Diagrams.
After p in entry
Association Class
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
An Association Class is a UML construct that enables an Association to have Attributes and Operations (features).
After p in entry
An Association Class is represented as a Class connected by a dotted line to a directed Association between two Classes.
Association Classes should NOT have Associations to any other Classes outside of this defined relationship.
After p in entry
Associations on Class Diagrams
After p in entry
Associations on Class Diagrams are used to represent the different types of relationship between Classes.
After p in entry
An Association is shown as a line between two Classes; it may also be labelled and indicate the direction in which it should be read. Notations may vary according to Association type.
Constraints may NOT be recorded against Associations.
After p in entry
Non-directional (or ‘Standard’) Association:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
A Non-directional (or ‘Standard’) Association on a Class Diagram denotes that the most basic form of relationship exists between elements.
After p in entry
A Non-directional Association is shown as a line between two classes; it can be read in either direction (so has no arrows to denote directionality).
Multiplicities may also be shown next to each element at the ends of an Association.
After p in entry
Directed Association:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
A Directed Association between Classes shows the direction of an Association (implying precedence).
The Association may be constrained by the Multiplicities of the Classes as represented on the Connector, in the direction of the arrow head.
After p in entry
A Directed Association is denoted by a line with an open arrowhead(s) between the Classes.
This Association type should also show the Multiplicity of each element at the ends of the Association.
After p in entry
Inheritance Association:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
An Inheritance Association on a Class Diagram allows Classes to share or ‘inherit’ the Attributes / Operations and Association relationships of a ‘parent’ Class, as well as for the ‘child’ class/es to specialize the ‘parent’ class.
Depending upon which direction the Association is read, it can be defined as either a ‘Generalization’ or a ‘Specialization’ Association.
After p in entry
Inheritance Associations are denoted by a solid line between the Classes with an ‘empty’ arrowhead at the ‘parent’ Class end.
Multiplicity is invalid for this association.
After p in entry
Aggregation Association:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
An Aggregation Association is a type of association that identifies an element as either containing or composed of other elements. It is used to show how more complex elements (aggregates) are built from a collection of simpler elements.
An Aggregation Association on a Class Diagram defines a relationship where Classes are ‘a part of’ a whole entity.
After p in entry
Aggregate Associations are represented by a line between Classes with a diamond at the ‘whole’ end of the Association.
Multiplicity may be shown at the ‘component’ Class end of the Association.
After p in entry
Composition Association:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
A Composition Association is a strict form of Aggregation. The ‘part’ can belong to only one Composite Aggregation at a time.
If the composite is deleted, all of its parts are deleted along with it.
After p in entry
Composite (Aggregate) Associations are denoted by a line between Classes with a filled or solid diamond at the ‘whole’ end of the line.
Multiplicity may be shown at the component class end of the Association
After p in entry
Class Multiplicities on Associations:
tablespacer
tablespacer
After p in entry
Class Multiplicities may be represented on the Association between Classes to show how many of each must / may exist for the relationship shown (except for Inheritance Associations).
After p in entry
Multiplicity is represented in the ‘n..n’ format (e.g. 1..*), and can be placed at either end of the Association.
Also see section: Multiplicities represented on Associations.
