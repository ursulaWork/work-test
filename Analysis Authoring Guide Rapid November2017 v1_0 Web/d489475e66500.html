Business Information Model Artefact (Adopted Notations)
Introduction oNext: Information Analysis Model (IAM) - (Product Description)
This section outlines the Adopted Notations for the Business Information Model (BIM) Artefact (for example figure see: BIM — example diagram):
• 
The BIM Artefact represents Class Elements and their Associations.
• 
These BIM Artefact specific adopted notations should also be read in conjunction with the Generic Class Model Artefacts Adopted Notations.
• 
BIM’s shall only be developed using the approved CASE Toolset or documentation approach.
• 
All Class Elements on a BIM must:
◦ 
Be named to represent the data / information concept being modelled.
◦ 
Have a brief description in the metadata notes as well as an alias (a numbering device that denotes that unique entity — this is usually achieved by the CASE Toolset, or documentation approach).
◦ 
When elaborated on a BIM Diagram all Class Elements must use the standard form of class element.
◦ 
Class Elements on BIM’s must not have attributes OR operations applied to them
• 
All Associations on a BIM must:
◦ 
Be named (explaining the reason for the relationship).
◦ 
Show ‘directed’ associations.
◦ 
Not have or show multiplicities / cardinalities.
◦ 
Not have any constraints applied to them.
• 
Whilst providing an overview of a domains information and business concepts, not all of the concepts represented on a BIM will necessarily be taken forward for analysis;
◦ 
The BIM aims to provide an overview of the domain to assist analysts and others to understand the wider context of the areas taken forward for analysis.
◦ 
Large or unwieldy BIM Diagrams can also be split, using ‘Composite’ Class Diagrams or Extract Diagrams to aid navigability / readability, as defined by the methodology being used.
• 
A BIM Artefact will probably be created in a workshop environment with stakeholders, it uses a basic subset of class modelling notation to support easier understanding:
◦ 
It may also be appropriate to create a BIM using preexisting domain or analysis artefacts, e.g. BIM, BPMs, and / or Stakeholder Business Model as input.
• 
The BIM should be created using business terminology, capturing concepts that are important and recognisable to business users. This is the reason why no constraints are applied to BIM classes.
• 
Where a class may ultimately represents a system, avoid referencing particular systems; i.e. use logical generic terms for example ‘orderingService’.
• 
All actors identified within the Use Case Model should be represented as entities within the BIM. Representation as a generalised class is acceptable. The BIM may also show additional actors which are not represented elsewhere to present a more complete overview of the domain.
• 
BIM’s are not intended to be definitive documents, so they will not capture the entire information requirement and associated flows; however it is intended that the content should be understandable and recognisable to business users.
• 
In accordance with the Methodology, the BIM is a formal publishable artefact. At the point of publication the BIM should be correct and consistent with the rest of the artefacts in the Model.
• 
A BIM diagram may be annotated using notes but these will be of a general business nature rather than representing formal elements such as constraints.
• 
Development of the BIM is iterative in nature and may be updated during the lifecycle of the project:
◦ 
When the BIM is first created, the focus of attention may be on a particular aspect of the domain. This may lead to the initial model being more developed in some areas than others. Subsequent work may shift the focus yet again onto different areas of the domain and move development onto those aspects of the model.
