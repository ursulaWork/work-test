Scoping Analysis Requirements
Scoping Analysis Requirements
The development of Requirements starts early in the lifecycle of a project; before embarking on an information gathering exercise attention should be given to establishing the scope of the envisaged analysis.
The development of the Domain Analysis Scope and Objectives Document in conjunction with the Stakeholder Business Model and Business Information Model together help to determine the scope of the analysis.  
The Domain Analysis Scope and Objectives Document should set the primary scope of the analysis, as well as the scope of any requirements gathering processes. The scope may be further confirmed during the development of the Stakeholder Business Model.  
Requirements also need to be negotiated and validated to deal with conflict, overlaps or unrealistic expectation.  Important decisions need to be taken as to which requirements are out of scope and these need to be documented in the analysis scope and objectives document.

Gathering Requirements
Gathering Requirements
Having a group of key stakeholders upon whom to draw is critical to supporting the activity of information gathering. These include domain experts, clinicians and users, as well as other analysts. These individuals should be able to provide relevant information or identify key reference sources. 
Requirements are sourced through a variety of consultations, with stakeholders and other domain experts. These consultations may take the form of workshops, interviews and questionnaires. 
Additional techniques:
Stakeholder Analysis and User Personas:
•  Help understand the different type of user or roles in the domain. 
•  The requirements related to these roles.
Scenarios: 
•  Help gather and refine Requirements in a user friendly way.
•  Encourage more creative and innovative thinking.
•  Encourage team review.
•  Ensure that Requirements are captured in an accessible and intuitive way.
•  Eases the process, by applying requirements to real-life situations.
Prototyping: 
Software prototypes can be constructed to visualise the system or part of it on order to obtain feedback. A prototype in itself could be consider a method documenting requirements and should be under configuration management inline with the project iterations.  
Sources of Information 
In addition to Stakeholder consultations and research or background reading, the following should also be taken into consideration as sources that may define specific Requirements:
•  Legal requirements.
•  Professional regulations.
•  Professional guidelines or standards.
•  NHS Digital corporate requirements.
•  Existing and / or projected application requirements and specifications.
•  National secondary use requirements.
External requirement references (such as NICE Guidelines) are large and by their nature are not designed to be used as a statement of requirements. Stakeholder support is needed to write these down as individual coherent requirements that directly relate to a domain / project.
