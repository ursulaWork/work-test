“A Product Backlog is an updatable master list of work items relating to the building of a product.”
Unlike a more formal ‘requirements document’ the Backlog is understood as a dynamic body of information and so needs to be managed continually.
Who
Responsible: Business Analyst and Product Owner.
Required: Core team.
Why (purpose)
To ensure that the Backlog remains populated with items that are relevant, detailed and estimated to a degree appropriate with their priority, and in keeping with current understanding of the project or product and its objectives. The object is to ensure a smooth and steady flow of items into development.
So that:
•  The team is aware of the priority and direction of the product function and features.
•  The Product Owner has ownership of business value delivered.
•  The Business Analyst is able to keep "on top" of the requirement type items that form a part of the backlog.
When
Discovery.
Continually throughout delivery.
Duration
Discovery: Large ongoing task.
During delivery: Ongoing task.
What
There are regular organised meetings as well as background activities carried outside the meetings.
The following tasks should be undertaken as part of Backlog Refinement meetings:
•  Creating new user stories in response to newly discovered needs.
•  Re-assessing the relative priority of stories.
•  Splitting user stories which are high priority but too coarse grained to fit in an upcoming iteration.
•  Removing user stories that no longer appear relevant
The Business Analyst ensures that any changes are reflected in the analysis model. They check and playback information with team member for clarity.
Developers activities are:
•  Assigning estimates to stories which have yet to receive one.
•  Correcting estimates in light of newly discovered information.
Outputs
Prioritised Backlog items.
Agile Framework
This is a Scrum approach, but can be used by the Business Analyst to manage requirements and use cases etc within a Rapid analysis project.
Hint:Not all user stories need to have been broken down to a fine-grained level at the onset of the project, or given detailed estimates; but it is important that at any moment a ‘sufficient’ number of stories should be ready for scheduling in the next few iterations.
