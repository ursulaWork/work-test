Requirements are single statements of need for what a product or service should be or do. Requirements and their related Artefact Set capture and articulate any system or service requirements that may in-turn place constraints upon any information concepts or systems being developed. Requirements provide valuable input for the development of all Analysis activities / artefacts as part of an Analysis Model. Requirements and the relationships between them are elaborated on Requirements Diagrams, as well as other related element types (e.g. Use Cases).
requirements element
requirements element
  An example of Requirements Elements and their notations elaborated on a Requirements Diagram
Requirements are captured and elaborated as part of the approved methodologies within the Analysis Model using a defined set of Requirements Artefacts (along with their Adopted Notations) and can be classified as either Functional or Non-Functional.
NOTE: - Requirements are developed according to an adopted modelling approach, which draws heavily on the CASE Toolset ((Enterprise Architect [EA) and therefore may not be universally applicable to all modelling contexts or toolsets.
The purpose of a Requirement is to identifying any necessary capabilities, characteristics, or qualities of a system in order for it to have value. It is therefore envisaged that the products developed during an analysis project would aim to realise those Requirements.
Further information is also available supporting the Development of Requirements and the Requirements Artefacts that elaborate them.
There are no core notations for Requirements and their elements and artefacts are wholly constructed according to the methodology-defined Requirements Elements and Requirements Diagram Adopted Notations.

Requirements for Rapid Methodologyspacer text
Requirements are single statements of need for what a product or service should be or do. Requirements and their related Artefact Set capture and articulate any system or service requirements that may in-turn place constraints upon any information concepts or systems being developed. Requirements provide valuable input for the development of all Analysis activities / artefacts as part of an Analysis Model.
In the context of agile projects, requirements are used to help understand the scope.   
Requirements can be expressed at differing level of details and focus. 
Business Requirements
Business Requirements are high-level requirements that express the objectives and desired outcomes of an organization.  They are typically defined in a business case or other statements by the product owner or sponsor. They attempt to articulate why the organization is spending money and resources on the project.  
Stakeholder Requirements
Stakeholder Requirements are statements of the stakeholders' needs and expectations and describe the features that must be met if the business requirements are to be fulfilled.   
 Hint: Agree with PM where these are documented, an option could be the analysis scope & definition document. 
Functional Requirements
Functional Requirements are the bridge between the business and technical teams and provide the definition of what the system must do for its users that will in turn meet the business goals.   
 Hint: Agree with the team the purpose of these requirement & who needs them.  In the context of an agile project detailed functional requirements can overlap with Use Cases and/or User Stories. 
Non-functional Requirements
Non -functional requirements  define constraints or criteria that impact the operation of a system.
For further details of the application of this classification upon Requirements. see the section: Non-functional Requirements Matrix.

Accuracy / Precision
After p in entry
Accuracy is the degree of closeness of a measured or calculated quantity to its actual (true) value. Closely related to precision, which is the standard deviation from a given value, also called tolerance (engineering), reproducibility or repeatability, the degree to which further measurements or calculations show the same or similar results.
Accuracy is how close is to the target. Precision is how close the results are to each other (grouping). This should be considered with respect to calculations and data captured / required by the system, especially where rounding etc. is conducted.

Resilience / Robustness / Stability / Fault tolerance
After p in entry
Resilience is the ability to provide and maintain an acceptable level of service in the face of faults and challenges to normal operation. Robustness is the quality of an algorithm, network, hardware, implementation, etc. being able to withstand stress (the behavioural tolerances in non standard circumstances or changes in environment / circumstance).
A system, organism or design may be said to be "robust" if it is capable of coping well with variations (sometimes unpredictable variations) in its operating environment with minimal damage, alteration or loss of functionality. Closely related to resilience.

The responsiveness of an interactive system describes how quickly it responds to certain events. Specific events and the associated responsiveness should be defined. This may be reaction to user input, but also to internal/external, system/non-system triggered events.
